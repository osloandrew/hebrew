Hebrew Pronunciation Guide for English Speakers

Hebrew has five basic vowel signs (niqqud) and a relatively small set of consonants, some of which do not exist in English. This guide uses IPA and notes important pronunciation distinctions between Modern Israeli Hebrew (the standard today) and traditional/liturgical Hebrew. Examples are shown with Hebrew script, niqqud (vowel points), and transliteration.

Stress in Hebrew

In Modern Hebrew, stress usually falls on the last syllable of a word, but many common words preserve older patterns with penultimate stress. Unlike Spanish, Hebrew does not use written accents to mark stress. Examples: ◊ë÷∑÷º◊ô÷¥◊™ [Ààbajit] (penultimate), ◊ú÷∏◊õ◊ï÷º◊ì [laÀàœáud] (final).

Dialect Notes at a Glance

Modern Israeli Hebrew: Standard in schools/media; guttural ◊¢ and ◊ó often weakened.
Sephardic tradition: Historical basis of modern pronunciation; clear distinction of vowels.
Ashkenazi Hebrew: Still used in prayer; stress often moves to penultimate syllable; some vowel shifts (e.g. kamatz ‚Üí /o/).
Mizrahi traditions: Preserve pharyngeal sounds [ƒß,  ï] for ◊ó and ◊¢.

Helpful Symbols and Conventions
